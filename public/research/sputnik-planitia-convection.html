Sputnik Planitia's nitrogen-ice convection cells

Review of New Horizons imaging of Sputnik Planitia, the polygonal nitrogen-ice cell pattern, the convection model derived from cell sizes and geological constraints, and what the dynamics imply for Pluto's interior heat budget.
2026-04-25 · 7 min read · Reviewed by Corgan Studio, Inc.

Introduction

Sputnik Planitia is a 1000 km wide nitrogen-ice plain occupying the western lobe of Tombaugh Regio on Pluto. Imaged at sub-kilometre resolution by New Horizons during the July 2015 flyby, the surface displays a network of bright polygonal cells with width 20 to 40 km, separated by darker grooves. The cell pattern, the absence of impact craters, and the geometry of the basin together provide the strongest case in the Solar System for active solid-state convection in volatile ice.

Geological setting

Sputnik Planitia sits within a 1000 by 1500 km basin approximately 4 km below Pluto's mean elevation. The basin is interpreted as an ancient impact structure subsequently filled by nitrogen, methane, and carbon-monoxide ice that condensed from the atmosphere and migrated equatorward over Pluto's 248-Earth-year orbital period. The basin's location near Pluto's anti-Charon point - 180 degrees longitude from the satellite's sub-point - is itself a consequence of the load: the dense ice fill drives a true polar wander that reorients Pluto until the basin lies along the tidal axis.

Polygonal cell pattern

The cell width is the diagnostic observable. Each polygon is bounded by narrow trough-like depressions that the New Horizons LORRI camera resolves as raised borders flanking shallow channels. Within each cell, the surface is smooth at LORRI resolution and free of impact craters. Crater absence places an upper limit on surface age of approximately 10 million years - extraordinarily young for a body 5.9 billion km from the Sun. The cell-bounding troughs correspond to downwelling sites in a convecting layer; the cell interiors correspond to upwelling.

Convection model

McKinnon et al. (2016) and Trowbridge et al. (2016) independently published convection models in Nature in June 2016. The cell-size selection rule for Rayleigh-Benard convection in a viscous fluid predicts cell width comparable to the depth of the convecting layer. Observed cell widths of 20 to 40 km therefore imply a nitrogen-ice convection layer 4 to 10 km thick. Below the convection layer, the nitrogen ice is predicted to be conductively stable.

Driving the convection requires both a thermal gradient and a sufficiently low effective viscosity. Pluto's interior heat flow, estimated from radiogenic heating of a chondritic rock fraction at 50 to 60 percent by mass, is approximately 3 milliwatts per square metre. This flow is sufficient to maintain the nitrogen-ice layer in the warm-base, viscous regime where solid-state convection is energetically possible. Nitrogen ice creep at temperatures near its melting point of 63 K is dominated by grain-boundary sliding and is several orders of magnitude weaker than water ice at equivalent homologous temperatures.

Cell evolution

Sputnik Planitia cells migrate. New Horizons identified secondary structures - sublimation pits, drifted ridges, surface streaks - that are organised relative to the cell-boundary network and indicate net horizontal flow rates of order centimetres per year. The estimated surface renewal timescale is hundreds of thousands to a few million years, consistent with the impact-crater absence. This makes Sputnik Planitia one of two known Solar-System surfaces with sustained renewal at present (Io's volcanic surface is the other). Triton's Cantaloupe terrain has been proposed as a third, but the New Horizons data set does not extend to Triton.

Implications for Pluto's interior

The presence of active nitrogen-ice convection requires the basin floor to be at temperatures within a few kelvin of the nitrogen melting point. Combined with the inferred ice-fill thickness, this places the base of the nitrogen layer near 50-60 K. The implied heat flux sets a lower bound on radiogenic and tidal heating that exceeds the contributions from chondritic radiogenics alone by a factor of 2 to 3. The most plausible additional source is residual primordial heat from accretion, plus the latent heat released by gradual freezing of a deep subsurface water-ammonia ocean. The inferred ocean is consistent with gravity- and shape-anomaly arguments based on Sputnik Planitia's location at the anti-Charon point.

Outlook

No follow-on mission to Pluto is in development. New Horizons remains the only direct dataset and continues outbound through the Kuiper Belt with telemetry expected through the 2030s. Telescopic monitoring of Pluto's atmosphere via stellar occultations by ground-based and SOFIA observers tracks seasonal volatile transport at decadal cadence. Laboratory rheology of nitrogen ice at Pluto-relevant temperatures and pressures continues to be the leading source of model refinement.
